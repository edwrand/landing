How does it work?

Many businesses struggle to collect high-quality feedback. Traditional methods involve sending mass survey emails, which only yield responses about 10-30% of the time. That's a lot of customers left out!

DataGift takes a different approach. Instead of casting a wide net, we target specific customers, creating a marketplace where you buy valuable feedback. For your business, it's about acquiring quality data; for customers, it's a reward for their honest opinions—a win-win.

As a business you would follow this process: 1. Create a detailed survey 2. Set a USD reward amount 3. Select your target audience 4. Launch your survey 5. Receive high-quality feedback 6. Reward your customers with cash

Example Use Cases

Case 1: SaaS Product Enhancement Company: A B2B software-as-a-service (SaaS) provider that offers project management tools. Problem: The company noticed a decline in customer retention and engagement but couldn’t identify the exact pain points through traditional surveys. Their response rates were low (about 15%), leaving them without actionable feedback. Solution: Using DataGift, they decided to reward customers with $5 for completing a 10-question survey about specific product features and pain points. The company targeted customers who had used the product for at least 6 months and had experienced certain features. Outcome: The survey achieved a 65% response rate, and the data revealed two major usability issues that the company was previously unaware of. With the detailed feedback, they implemented product updates that improved retention by 25% over the following quarter. The $5 incentive made customers feel valued, and many participants continued to engage with the product updates.


Case 2: Retail Product Launch Company: An online clothing retailer planning to launch a new sustainable fashion line. Problem: The retailer needed feedback on customer preferences for colors, designs, and pricing but had struggled to gather meaningful insights from their email list, which typically generated a response rate of 12%. Solution: Through DataGift, the company created a survey targeting previous customers who had shown interest in eco-friendly products. They set a reward of $10 for completing a detailed survey on preferred materials, designs, and pricing structures for their upcoming product line. Outcome: The targeted audience responded enthusiastically, with a 70% response rate. Customers shared valuable feedback, helping the company choose the top 3 most desired product designs and optimal pricing points. As a result, the company launched a collection that sold out within the first two weeks, leading to a 40% increase in revenue from the previous quarter. The incentive strategy made customers feel more engaged and connected to the brand.


Case 3: Hospitality Customer Experience Improvement Company: A luxury hotel chain looking to improve its guest experience, particularly around its spa and wellness services. Problem: The hotel had previously relied on post-stay email surveys, which yielded a low response rate of 18%. They struggled to understand why guests weren’t utilizing the spa services as much as expected. Solution: Through DataGift, the hotel targeted guests who had booked a spa package but didn’t use all the services. They created a detailed survey offering a $20 incentive for participants to share their reasons for not using the spa. This survey specifically targeted guests within 1 month of their stay. Outcome: The hotel saw a 60% response rate, with feedback indicating that many guests found the spa booking process confusing. The hotel made key adjustments to its service flow and saw a 35% increase in spa bookings within the next two months. The incentive built goodwill with their high-value guests, leading to higher loyalty and repeat bookings.

Why Pay Customers for Their Feedback?

1. Increased Response Rates: Monetary incentives significantly increase participation in surveys.

2. Cost-Effective Data Collection: Offering incentives may reduce overall costs by speeding up data collection.

3. Better Data Quality Without Bias: Incentivizing participants does not compromise the quality of responses.

4. Broader Demographic Representation: Paying for feedback attracts a more diverse and representative pool of respondents.

5. Strengthening Customer Relationships: Offering incentives signals that customer opinions are valued, fostering loyalty and trust.
